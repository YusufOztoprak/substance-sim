Mathematical Models

Substance Sim uses advanced pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic models to simulate biological processes accurately.

1. Pharmacokinetics: The Bateman Function

Unlike simple exponential decay, real substances take time to be absorbed into the bloodstream. We use the Bateman Function to model both absorption and elimination phases simultaneously.

$$ C(t) = \frac{D \cdot F \cdot k_a}{V_d (k_a - k_e)} \left( e^{-k_e t} - e^{-k_a t} \right) $$
  • $ C(t) $: Concentration in blood plasma at time $ t $
  • $ D $: Dose administered
  • $ F $: Bioavailability (fraction of dose that reaches systemic circulation)
  • $ k_a $: Absorption rate constant
  • $ k_e $: Elimination rate constant (derived from half-life $ t_{1/2} $)
  • $ V_d $: Volume of distribution

2. Pharmacodynamics: The Hill Equation

The biological effect is not linear. It follows a sigmoidal curve, meaning there is a threshold for effect and a saturation point where higher doses don't increase the effect significantly.

$$ E = \frac{E_{max} \cdot C^n}{EC_{50}^n + C^n} $$
  • $ E $: Effect intensity (0-100)
  • $ E_{max} $: Maximum achievable effect
  • $ EC_{50} $: Concentration required to achieve 50% of maximum effect
  • $ n $: Hill coefficient (slope factor)

3. Neuroadaptation (Tolerance)

Repeated exposure leads to tolerance, reducing the drug's efficacy over time. We model this as a function of Cumulative Exposure (Area Under the Curve - AUC).

$$ Tolerance(t) = 1 - e^{-\alpha \cdot \int_{0}^{t} C(\tau) d\tau} $$

As the cumulative exposure increases, the tolerance factor approaches 1, dampening the effect of subsequent doses.

4. Multi-Dose Superposition

When multiple doses are taken, the concentration at any time $ t $ is the sum of the residual concentrations of all previous doses.

$$ C_{total}(t) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} C_i(t - t_i) $$
